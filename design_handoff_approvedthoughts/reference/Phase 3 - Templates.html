Feeling the AGI

I have been using LLMs daily for two years now, and something shifted this spring. The models stopped feeling like clever autocomplete and started feeling like a counterpart — someone who reads what I send, thinks for a moment, and writes back.1

The shift wasn't about benchmarks. The benchmarks have been climbing all year. It was about a different kind of evidence — the kind that accrues quietly, in the small moments where a model surprises me by understanding what I actually meant rather than what I literally said.

Three small surprises

I keep a folder of these moments. Here are three from the last week.

The first was a planning conversation. I was thinking out loud about a book I want to write, and the model interrupted me — politely, but firmly — to point out that the question I was asking presupposed an answer to a different question I hadn't actually thought through yet.2 It was right.

The second was a debugging session that lasted forty-five minutes. I was stuck on a subtle bug in a build script. The model and I went back and forth, each turn narrowing the search. When we finally found it — a wrong path separator on Windows — the model said something like, "I should have asked about your operating system earlier. I'm sorry for the long detour." No human collaborator I have ever worked with apologizes that crisply.

The third was the smallest. I asked for help proofreading a paragraph and the model said, "This paragraph is fine, but the one before it is doing the same work twice. Want me to merge them?" I had not asked it to look at the paragraph before. It had read the whole essay on its own, the way a good editor would.

What this isn't

None of this is AGI. The models still hallucinate. They still get math wrong sometimes. They still fail in ways that would embarrass a careful undergraduate. But the texture of the daily interaction is different than it was a year ago, and I think that is the thing worth paying attention to.

Benchmarks measure what we know to ask. The real shifts are in what we did not know to measure.

If I had to name the change in one sentence: the models have stopped feeling like tools and started feeling like collaborators with a particular set of strengths and weaknesses, the way colleagues do.

Where I think this goes

The next step is not "AGI" in the dramatic sense. It is something more boring and more profound — the slow, distributed integration of these systems into how knowledge work actually gets done. Not a single moment of takeoff, but a thousand small moments where something that used to take an hour now takes ten minutes.3

I am writing this slowly because I want to remember what it felt like before the shift was obvious to everyone.

Footnotes
  1. "Counterpart" is doing a lot of work in this sentence. I do not mean a peer. I mean something more like a thoughtful research assistant who has read very widely and forgets nothing of what we have already discussed in this session. ↩
  2. The specific question was about whether the book is for people who already love the topic or for people who do not yet know they should. Different books. ↩
  3. I keep thinking of the line about how the future is already here, just unevenly distributed. The unevenness is the interesting part. ↩

About

I am Apoorv. I am a product person living in Bengaluru, with stops in Boston, San Francisco, and Bombay along the way. This is my notebook — a place where I think out loud about the things I read, the books I am working through, and the slow questions that don't have a fast answer.

The site has four kinds of writing:

Links — a weekly digest of articles, essays, and threads that made me stop. Curated, not exhaustive. Roughly half a dozen items per week, with a sentence or two on why each one matters.

Posts — longer-form essays on topics I am thinking through. These take me a while to write, so they appear unevenly.

Notes — short, dated observations. Not edited heavily. The fast loop.

Thought Pieces — when a Note grows up. Quieter than a Post; more personal than a Note.

Why "Approved Thoughts"

The name is a small joke at my own expense. Earlier in my career, I had a manager who would mark up draft documents with the phrase "approved thought" next to ideas she found worth pursuing. It became a private shorthand for the thing I am still trying to do — distinguish the ideas that are merely interesting from the ones that are actually true, and worth the trouble of saying out loud.

How this site works

Static HTML, generated from Markdown by a small Node script I wrote myself. Hosted on a CDN, no tracking, no cookies, no comments. The full source is on GitHub. If you find a typo, a pull request is the most flattering form of feedback.

If you want to follow along, the RSS feed is the best way. I do not have a newsletter and do not plan to start one.

RSS feed — embedded content fix

Build-side note. Not a design surface.

The problem: right now your RSS feed strips out tweets, YouTube embeds, and inline HTML — readers see only plain text or broken markup.

The fix (build-side, not design): in build.js, when generating feed.xml, run the same Markdown→HTML pipeline you use for post pages, then wrap the resulting HTML in <content:encoded><![CDATA[ … ]]></content:encoded> per RSS spec. Most readers (Feedly, NetNewsWire, Reeder, Inoreader) honor CDATA-wrapped HTML and will render images, iframes, and styled blockquotes inline.

Two caveats: (1) some feed readers strip iframes for security — your YouTube embeds may show a fallback link instead of the player; provide a styled <a> with the video title as the fallback. (2) use absolute URLs for everything (images especially) since the feed is read out of context.
